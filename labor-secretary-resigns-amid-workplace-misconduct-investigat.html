Labor Secretary Resigns Amid Workplace Misconduct Investigation While Leading Historic Workforce Reduction

Department that lost 25% of staff under workplace violations probe gets new leader.

By Staff Reporter, Bureau of Government Affairs
INVALID DATE  ·  GOVMENT.ORG

Govment.org photo illustration  |  INVALID DATE

Labor Secretary Lori Chavez-DeRemer submitted her resignation Tuesday following a months-long internal investigation into alleged workplace misconduct at the Department of Labor, which has overseen a 25% reduction in its enforcement workforce during her tenure. The Bureau of Workplace Standards confirmed that investigators had documented multiple violations of federal employment law within the agency tasked with enforcing those same regulations.

"The irony is not lost on us," said an unnamed official from the Bureau of Administrative Efficiency, speaking on condition of anonymity. "We've achieved unprecedented streamlining of our workforce oversight capabilities while simultaneously demonstrating why such oversight might be necessary." The official noted that the department's complaint hotline had been disconnected in January due to budget constraints and lack of staff to answer calls.

Internal documents obtained through a Freedom of Information Act request reveal that the Department of Labor had eliminated 847 workplace safety inspectors, 312 wage and hour investigators, and 156 discrimination case officers since Chavez-DeRemer took office. The same period saw a 340% increase in internal HR violations, according to the Bureau of Statistical Contradictions.

The investigation reportedly began when employees filed complaints about mandatory unpaid overtime, unsafe working conditions in the department's own offices, and allegations of retaliation against whistleblowers. "It's a beautiful case study in governmental efficiency," explained Dr. Margaret Thornfield from the Institute for Bureaucratic Excellence. "They've eliminated the people who would normally investigate these exact types of violations."

Acting Secretary Janet Morrison announced that the remaining 12-person enforcement division would conduct a comprehensive review of workplace practices across all American businesses. "We're confident that our skeleton crew can handle the regulatory oversight of 160 million workers," Morrison stated during a press conference held via Zoom from her car due to office space limitations.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics confirmed that workplace injury reports have declined 89% nationwide since 2025, though officials acknowledged this may be related to the elimination of the reporting mechanisms rather than actual safety improvements. The department's final inspector retired last month without replacement, citing "philosophical differences with the mission."

President Trump praised Chavez-DeRemer's tenure, noting her success in "right-sizing government bloat while maintaining the highest standards of workplace excellence." The White House announced that Morrison would continue in an acting capacity indefinitely, as the Senate confirmation process for a permanent replacement could take up to eighteen months given current staffing levels at relevant oversight committees.

The Labor Department's main building in Washington will be converted to a co-working space for small businesses, with the remaining staff relocated to a rented conference room at a nearby Holiday Inn Express.
